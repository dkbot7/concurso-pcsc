1. TEXT INTERPRETATION — Most tested topic

What the exam requires

The exam provides a text (newspaper article, chronicle, opinion piece) and asks questions about its content. Your task is to understand exactly what the author wrote — nothing more, nothing less.

Golden Rule: What is NOT in the text does NOT exist. Never generalize, exaggerate, or invent. Always return to the text.

Common traps

TrapExample
OvergeneralizationText says "many" → wrong answer says "all"
Going beyond the textAnswer adds information not present in the text
DistortionAnswer changes the author's intent
Absolute negationText is conditional → answer states it as absolute

2. COHERENCE AND COHESION

Coherence

Coherence is about ideas making logical sense together. A coherent text does not contradict itself. All ideas flow in a connected, logical way from beginning to end.

Cohesion

Cohesion is the technical connection between sentences using pronouns, conjunctions, and transitional expressions.

Cohesion elementFunctionExamples
PronounsReplace nouns to avoid repetitionhe, she, it, they, this, that
ConjunctionsConnect clauses and ideasbut, because, therefore, if
AdverbsConnect ideas temporally/logicallyhowever, furthermore, meanwhile

3. VERBAL AGREEMENT

The verb must agree with the subject in number (singular/plural) and person.

Basic rule

Singular subject → singular verb: "The officer arrived."
Plural subject → plural verb: "The officers arrived."

Tricky cases

CaseRuleExample
Subject after verbStill agrees with subject"There were clues in the file."
Collective nounsSingular verb"The majority passed."
Compound subjectPlural verb"The detective and the expert arrived."
Tip: Find the subject first, then match the verb to it — regardless of word order.

4. CONJUNCTIONS

Conjunctions connect ideas. Their type defines the logical relationship between clauses.

TypeConjunctionsExample
Oppositionbut, however, yet, nevertheless"I studied hard, but still had doubts."
Causebecause, since, as, given that"She passed because she studied."
Conclusiontherefore, thus, so, consequently"She studied; therefore she passed."
Conditionif, unless, provided that"If you study, you will pass."
Purposeso that, in order to"I study so that I can be approved."

5. VERB MOODS AND DOUBLE PARTICIPLE

Three verb moods

MoodUseExample
IndicativeCertain, real facts"I study every day."
SubjunctiveUncertainty, hypothesis, desire"I hope she passes."
ImperativeOrders, requests, advice"Study every day!"

Double Participle (Portuguese grammar)

Some Portuguese verbs have two past participle forms. The choice depends on the auxiliary verb:

InfinitiveRegular (with ter/haver)Irregular (with ser/estar)
prender (to arrest)prendidopreso
acender (to light)acendidoaceso
entregar (to deliver)entregadoentregue
aceitar (to accept)aceitadoaceito
eleger (to elect)elegidoeleito
Rule: With TER and HAVER → use regular. With SER and ESTAR → use irregular.

PRACTICE QUESTIONS

Q1. The exam text says "some officers were present." A correct interpretation would be:

  • A) All officers were at the location
  • B) A portion of the officers were present
  • C) No officer was absent
  • D) Officers were forced to be present
Answer: B — "some" means a portion, not all. Never generalize beyond what the text states.

Q2. The sentence "She studied hard; _______ she passed the exam" requires a conjunction of:

  • A) Opposition
  • B) Cause
  • C) Conclusion
  • D) Condition
Answer: C — "therefore / thus" = conclusion. The second clause is the result of the first.

Q3. Choose the correct use of the double participle: "The evidence had been _______ by the forensic team."

  • A) entregado
  • B) entregue
  • C) Both are correct here
  • D) Neither is correct
Answer: B — "had been" uses SER → irregular form "entregue."
